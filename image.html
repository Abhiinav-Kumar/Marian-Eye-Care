Glaucoma Services


Glaucoma services focus on the early detection, diagnosis, and management of glaucoma—a group of eye conditions that damage the optic nerve and can lead to permanent vision loss if left untreated. These services include comprehensive eye examinations, measurement of intraocular pressure, visual field testing, and optic nerve imaging to monitor changes over time. Treatment options may involve medicated eye drops, laser therapy, or surgery to reduce eye pressure and prevent further damage. Since glaucoma often progresses without noticeable symptoms, regular check-ups are crucial, especially for those at higher risk. With timely intervention and continuous care, glaucoma services help preserve vision and maintain a good quality of life.

• OCT RNFL (Retinal Nerve Fiber Layer): Measures the thickness of the retinal nerve fibers.
• Anterior Segment OCT: Evaluates the front part of the eye including the cornea and angle.
• Central Corneal Thickness (CCT): Helps assess glaucoma risk by measuring the cornea’s thickness.
• Hood Field Test: An advanced visual field test that checks peripheral vision and optic nerve damage.

Glaucoma Evaluation


Glaucoma evaluation is a comprehensive process used to detect and assess glaucoma, an eye condition that can cause irreversible vision loss due to damage to the optic nerve. The evaluation includes a series of tests such as measuring intraocular pressure (IOP), examining the optic nerve for signs of damage, conducting visual field tests to assess peripheral vision, and using imaging technologies like OCT (Optical Coherence Tomography) to examine the structure of the retina and optic nerve. These tests help in early detection and monitoring of the disease, especially since glaucoma often progresses without noticeable symptoms. A thorough glaucoma evaluation ensures timely diagnosis and the development of an effective treatment plan to prevent vision loss and protect long-term eye health. Regular monitoring is essential for managing the condition and preserving vision.

• OCT RNFL (Retinal Nerve Fiber Layer): Measures the thickness of the retinal nerve fibers.
• Anterior Segment OCT: Evaluates the front part of the eye including the cornea and angle.
• Central Corneal Thickness (CCT): Helps assess glaucoma risk by measuring the cornea’s thickness.
• Hood Field Test: An advanced visual field test that checks peripheral vision and optic nerve damage.

OCT Scan for All Retinal Diseases


OCT (Optical Coherence Tomography) scan is a non-invasive imaging technique used to diagnose and monitor various retinal diseases with high precision. It provides detailed cross-sectional images of the retina, allowing eye specialists to detect conditions such as diabetic retinopathy, macular degeneration, retinal detachment, and macular holes at an early stage. This advanced technology helps in assessing the thickness and structure of retinal layers, ensuring accurate diagnosis and effective treatment planning. OCT scans are quick, painless, and essential for the ongoing management of retinal diseases, contributing to better vision outcomes and long-term eye health. Regular OCT screenings are especially important for patients with chronic eye conditions, helping to prevent vision loss through timely intervention and care.

• Diabetic Retinopathy
• Age-related Macular Degeneration (AMD)
• Macular Holes
• Retinal Detachment
• Other retinal abnormalities

YAG Laser Treatment


YAG Laser Treatment is a specialized procedure used to improve vision in patients who develop cloudiness behind the intraocular lens after cataract surgery, a condition known as posterior capsule opacification (PCO). This painless, outpatient treatment uses a YAG (yttrium aluminum garnet) laser to create a small opening in the cloudy capsule, restoring clear vision almost immediately. The procedure is quick, safe, and highly effective, usually completed within minutes without the need for any incisions. YAG laser treatment helps enhance visual clarity and reduces the need for glasses or other corrective measures. It offers long-lasting results with minimal recovery time, allowing patients to resume normal activities the same day. Regular eye check-ups after the procedure help ensure stable vision and monitor overall eye health.

• Posterior Capsular Opacification (PCO): Common after cataract surgery; YAG laser clears the clouded lens capsule.
• Peripheral Iridotomy: A preventive treatment for angle-closure glaucoma, creating a small opening in the iris.

Green Laser Treatment


Green Laser Treatment, also known as selective laser trabeculoplasty (SLT), is a non-invasive procedure used to treat glaucoma by reducing intraocular pressure (IOP). This treatment uses a green-colored laser to target the trabecular meshwork, the drainage system of the eye, to improve fluid outflow and lower eye pressure. It is typically recommended for patients with open-angle glaucoma, especially when medication alone is not enough to control pressure. The procedure is quick, painless, and requires no incisions, offering significant benefits in managing glaucoma without the need for long-term medication. Green Laser Treatment is a safe and effective option that helps preserve vision by controlling eye pressure and preventing further optic nerve damage. Regular follow-up appointments are essential to monitor the success of the treatment and ensure optimal eye health.

• Diabetic Macular Oedema: Swelling in the macula due to diabetes.
• Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy: Advanced stage of diabetic eye disease involving abnormal blood vessel growth.
• Central Serous Retinopathy (CSR): A condition where fluid builds under the retina, leading to vision distortion.

Intravitreal Injections


Intravitreal injections are a common treatment for various serious eye conditions, particularly those affecting the retina, such as age-related macular degeneration (AMD), diabetic retinopathy, and retinal vein occlusion. The procedure involves injecting medication directly into the vitreous, the gel-like substance inside the eye. These medications, such as anti-VEGF (vascular endothelial growth factor) drugs, help reduce inflammation, prevent abnormal blood vessel growth, and control fluid leakage in the retina, which can lead to vision loss. Intravitreal injections are generally well-tolerated, though patients may experience mild discomfort or a temporary increase in pressure after the procedure. Regular treatments may be required, depending on the condition being treated, to stabilize or improve vision. Follow-up care ensures the effectiveness of the treatment and the monitoring of any potential side effects.

• For Diabetes: Helps reduce swelling, manage diabetic macular oedema and other complications.
• For Age-related Macular Degeneration (AMD): Slows down progression and preserves vision.
